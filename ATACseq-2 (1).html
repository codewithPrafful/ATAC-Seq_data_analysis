The “Per base sequence quality” is an integral component of quality control in next-generation sequencing (NGS) data analysis, frequently assessed using tools like FastQC. This metric offers a visual depiction of quality scores across all bases in a set of sequencing reads. As indicated below, all 16 samples have passed this quality check.

c) What does the “Per base sequence content” tell you? Are there any samples that fail this check? If so, why?

The "Per base sequence content" metric in tools like FastQC provides insights into the nucleotide composition (A, T, G, C) at each base position across all sequencing reads in a sample. If the base content at each position is not evenly distributed and the proportion of specific bases (A, T, G, C) is abnormally high or low, it could indicate sequencing errors or issues in the sample preparation process. When the Per Base Sequence Content appears poor across all data, it is likely because Tn5 transposase tends to preferentially cut at specific nucleotide sequences. This can result in an overrepresentation of certain bases at the beginning of the sequences.

  • MERGED LIB: Focuses on combining multiple sequencing libraries from a single sample to enhance data coverage and depth. It aims to provide a comprehensive dataset by integrating all sequencing data from different runs for the same sample.

It aims to validate findings by integrating data from replicates, ensuring that results are consistent and reproducible across multiple experiments.

1. Summarize signals over regions of interest

b) Combine computeMatrix with plotHeatmap/plotProfile from deepTools2 to generate plots. What do you observe? Explain your findings from a gene-regulation perspective.

ATAC-seq data can only reveal chromatin accessibility, so we aimed to obtain meaningful results by comparing it with other data. Additionally, we aimed to analyze the gene activation levels by comparing the data from day 14.5 and day 15.5.

We created plotProfiles using deepTools2 with the positional information of promoters and TSS marked on the genome from enhancers from the ChIP-seq segmentation, promoters based on the gene annotation, and chromatin accessible peaks, as well as the histone signals from ChIP-seq experiments in those regions. The parts marked as Center represent enhancer regions. The signals of H3K4me1 and H3K27ac, associated with active enhancers, showed high activity in both the enhancers from the ChIP-seq segmentation and the chromatin accessible peaks. This indicates that the genes are activated, and the signals were higher on day 15.5 compared to day 14.5, confirming that the genes were more activated on day 15.5.

Moreover, the signals of H3K4me3 and H3K27ac, associated with active promoters, showed activity in both the promoters based on gene annotation and chromatin accessible peaks. This also indicates that the genes are in an active state, and again, the signals were higher on day 15.5 compared to day 14.5, confirming that the genes were more activated on day 15.5. The reason the signals are higher after the TSS in the promoter-related graphs is due to the +1 nucleosome phenomenon.
